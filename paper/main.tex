% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Automated retrieval of terrain imagery from the HiRISE Mars Reconnaissance
Orbiter archive is fundamental to scientific query answering and mission
planning, yet existing deep learning approaches frame terrain similarity as
classification rather than ranked retrieval.
We present \qts{} (Quantum Terrain Similarity), a quantum metric
learning architecture applied to planetary terrain data.
The core contribution is an \emph{entangled-pair} Parametric Quantum Circuit
(PQC) used as a Siamese similarity head: given two CNN embeddings
$\mathbf{e}_a, \mathbf{e}_b \in \mathbb{S}^{N-1}$, the circuit applies
interleaved $\RX(\pi e_a^{(i)})$/$\RY(\pi e_b^{(i)})$ rotations on each
wire~$i$, jointly encoding both embeddings before any entangling gate is
applied.
Local Pauli-$\PauliZ$ measurements suppress barren plateaus following
Cerezo~et~al.~\cite{cerezo2021costbarren}.
The pipeline is trained end-to-end with triplet loss and domain-specific
hard negative mining targeting the geologically confusable class pairs in
HiRISE~v3.
A five-factor ablation (head type, encoding strategy, circuit depth, hard
negative mining, qubit count) on HiRISE~v3 (73,031 patches, 8 terrain
classes) shows that the default configuration ($N{=}8$, $L{=}2$) achieves
macro-MAP@10 = 0.533 on the held-out test split, with $L{=}3$ reaching
0.556.
The encoding strategy accounts for the largest performance gap
($\Delta{=}0.061$ over SLIQ-style sequential encoding~\cite{silver2023sliq}).
A ResNet50 ImageNet-pretrained strong baseline achieves macro-MAP@10 of
only 0.378, underscoring the value of domain-specific metric learning for
minority planetary terrain classes.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The HiRISE (High Resolution Imaging Science Experiment) camera aboard
NASA's Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter produces sub-meter resolution imagery
of the Martian surface at 25\,cm/pixel, generating a continuously expanding
archive of surface observations annotated with eight geomorphic terrain
classes: craters, bright and dark dunes, slope streaks, swiss cheese,
spiders, impact ejecta, and a general ``other'' category
\cite{wagstaff2018deepmars,wagstaff2021mars}.
Deep learning classifiers trained on this dataset have reached 96\%
top-1 accuracy \cite{wagstaff2021mars}, and operational deployment at
JPL's Planetary Data System has accelerated terrain cataloguing at scale
\cite{wagstaff2018deepmars}.

However, classification accuracy is a poor surrogate for the operationally
critical task: \emph{given a query terrain patch, rank all archived
observations by visual similarity}.
Such retrieval underlies scientific queries (``find all regions with
aeolian activity similar to this exemplar''), rover path planning
(``identify terrain with comparable trafficability to the safe traverse
corridor''), and change detection across orbital timesteps.
Retrieval requires learning a \emph{similarity function}, not a class
boundary.

Quantum metric learning offers a structurally motivated approach to this
problem: circuits naturally express inter-feature correlations that are
exponentially expensive to represent classically, and pairwise quantum
similarity measures capture geometric structure missed by linear functions~\cite{lloyd2020embeddings,schuld2019featurehilbert}.
The domain also poses a fine-grained challenge—bright and dark dunes are
compositionally distinct but geometrically similar; spider and swiss\_cheese
formations are visually confusable at patch scale—demanding a similarity
function that resolves these distinctions.
Yet despite growing activity in quantum image classification
\cite{mari2020transfer} and quantum Siamese learning \cite{silver2023sliq},
quantum similarity functions have not been systematically studied for
planetary terrain retrieval.

\subsection{Contributions}

We present \qts{}, a hybrid classical–quantum architecture with the
following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Quantum similarity learning applied to planetary terrain
      retrieval.}
      We establish retrieval metrics (MAP@10, Recall@$K$) on the
      HiRISE~v3 benchmark using a quantum head, providing a foundation for
      future quantum-classical comparison on planetary imagery.

\item \textbf{Entangled-pair encoding via interleaved \boldmath$\RX$/$\RY$
      rotations.}
      On each qubit~$i$, $\RX(\pi e_a^{(i)})$ then $\RY(\pi e_b^{(i)})$
      jointly encode both embeddings via non-commuting rotations; the
      $[\RX,\RY]\neq0$ nonlinearity produces a per-qubit state that is a
      nonlinear joint function of both inputs, contrasted with SLIQ's
      sequential encoding blocks~\cite{silver2023sliq}.

\item \textbf{Barren plateau mitigation via local observables.}
      Local Pauli-$\PauliZ$ measurements yield polynomial (vs.\ exponential)
      gradient decay with system size~\cite{cerezo2021costbarren}; no
      plateau fallback was triggered at $N=8$.

\item \textbf{Domain-specific hard negative mining.}
      Negatives target geologically confusable pairs (bright\_dune/dark\_dune,
      spider/swiss\_cheese) identified by pixel-space class-mean similarity;
      ablation A4 isolates the contribution.

\item \textbf{Five-factor ablation.}
      Varying head type (A1), encoding (A2), depth (A3), mining (A4), and
      qubit count (A5) provides a controlled account of each design decision.
\end{enumerate}


% ── 2. Related Work ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Mars terrain recognition.}
Wagstaff et~al.~\cite{wagstaff2018deepmars} introduced the HiRISE labeled
dataset and deployed AlexNet classification within NASA's Planetary Data
System; the AAAI~2021 follow-up~\cite{wagstaff2021mars} reached 96\%
top-1 accuracy on HiRISE~v3 over three operational years.
SPOC~\cite{rothrock2016spoc} extended terrain recognition to rover NAVCAM
imagery; Palafox et~al.~\cite{palafox2017automated} applied CNNs to HiRISE
landform detection.
None of these works train a dedicated similarity or retrieval function.

\textbf{Quantum machine learning and image similarity.}
Biamonte et~al.~\cite{biamonte2017qml} established the theoretical
foundations of QML.
Havl\'{i}\v{c}ek et~al.~\cite{havlicek2019supervised} demonstrated
quantum kernel SVMs on hardware; Schuld and
Killoran~\cite{schuld2019featurehilbert} formalized the equivalence between
PQC encoders and quantum kernels.
Mari et~al.~\cite{mari2020transfer} validated the hybrid CNN+PQC design
paradigm for image tasks.
SLIQ~\cite{silver2023sliq} is the closest prior work: it trains a quantum
Siamese circuit for image similarity using sequential encoding and
contrastive loss.
\qts{} differs by using interleaved encoding, triplet loss with domain-specific
hard mining, and local Pauli-$\PauliZ$ measurements to suppress barren
plateaus~\cite{cerezo2021costbarren,mcclean2018barren}.

\textbf{Classical metric learning.}
Schroff et~al.~\cite{schroff2015facenet} introduced triplet loss with
online hard negative mining; Koch et~al.~\cite{koch2015siamese} established
Siamese networks for one-shot recognition.
Harwood et~al.~\cite{harwood2017smartmining} and Suh
et~al.~\cite{suh2019stochastic} refined mining via embedding-space
proximity; our terrain-class-aware mining adapts these insights to HiRISE.

% ── 3. Background ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

A PQC on $N$ qubits applies parameterized unitaries $U(\bm{\theta})$ to
$|0\rangle^{\otimes N}$.
We use \emph{angle encoding}~\cite{schuld2020circuitcentric}: features
$e_i\!\in\![-1,1]$ map to rotation angles $\theta_i = \pi e_i$.
Local expectation values $f_i = \langle\psi|\PauliZ_i|\psi\rangle\in[-1,1]$
serve as outputs; with $N{=}8$ and $L{=}2$ StronglyEntanglingLayers
\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}, the ansatz has $3NL{=}48$ trainable parameters.

For metric learning, the triplet loss~\cite{schroff2015facenet} over anchor
$\mathbf{x}_a$, positive $\mathbf{x}_p$, and negative $\mathbf{x}_n$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[
    \max\!\bigl(0,\;(1-s_{ap}) - (1-s_{an}) + m\bigr)
  \right],
  \label{eq:triplet}
\end{equation}
where $s_{ap},s_{an}\!\in\!(0,1)$ are similarity scores and $m>0$ is a margin.

% ── 4. Proposed Method ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Proposed Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}

\qts{} follows a Siamese architecture: a shared CNN encoder
$\phi\colon \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{S}^{N-1}$ maps each terrain patch to
a unit-sphere embedding, and a quantum similarity head
$\psi\colon \mathbb{S}^{N-1}\times\mathbb{S}^{N-1}\to (0,1)$ produces a
scalar similarity score.
During training, triplets $(\mathbf{x}_a, \mathbf{x}_p, \mathbf{x}_n)$
are processed as
$(s_{ap}, s_{an}) =
(\psi(\phi(\mathbf{x}_a),\phi(\mathbf{x}_p)),\;
\psi(\phi(\mathbf{x}_a),\phi(\mathbf{x}_n)))$
and the pair is passed to~\eqref{eq:triplet}.
Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} illustrates the full pipeline.

\subsection{Classical CNN Encoder}
\label{sec:encoder}

The encoder $\phi$ consists of two $3{\times}3$ conv layers
(1$\to$32 and 32$\to$64 channels, each with MaxPool and ReLU),
a fully connected layer to 128 units, and a final projection to $N$
dimensions followed by $\ell_2$-normalization:
$\phi(\mathbf{x})\in\mathbb{S}^{N-1}$.
Both Siamese branches share all weights.
Weights use Kaiming normal initialization~\cite{he2015kaiming}.

\subsection{Quantum Similarity Head}
\label{sec:qhead}

\subsubsection{Entangled-Pair Encoding}
\label{sec:encoding}

Let $\mathbf{e}_a = \phi(\mathbf{x}_a)$ and
$\mathbf{e}_b = \phi(\mathbf{x}_b)$ be the two encoder outputs.
On qubit~$i$, we apply
\begin{equation}
  |\psi_i\rangle = \RY\!\left(\pi e_b^{(i)}\right)
                   \RX\!\left(\pi e_a^{(i)}\right)|0\rangle.
  \label{eq:encoding}
\end{equation}
Because $[\RX(\alpha), \RY(\beta)] \neq 0$ for generic $\alpha,\beta$,
the per-qubit state is a \emph{nonlinear joint function} of both
$e_a^{(i)}$ and $e_b^{(i)}$—the entangling ansatz therefore operates on
qubits already conditioned on both embeddings simultaneously, rather than
on each independently as in sequential encoding.

\textbf{Contrast with SLIQ.}
SLIQ~\cite{silver2023sliq} applies $\RZ(\pi e_a^{(i)})$ on all wires,
then $\RX(\pi e_b^{(i)})$ in a second block with no intervening entangling
gates, so the ansatz sees each embedding's effect independently.
Ablation A2 quantifies the retrieval gap from this difference.

\subsubsection{Variational Ansatz}

Following the encoding layer, $L$ StronglyEntanglingLayers~\cite{bergholm2018pennylane}
are applied ($\RZ$-$\RY$-$\RZ$ rotations with CZ entangling gates);
at $N{=}8$, $L{=}2$, the ansatz has $3NL=48$ parameters
$\bm{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^{48}$.

\subsubsection{Measurements and Barren Plateau Mitigation}

We measure the local expectation values
$f_i = \langle\psi(\bm{\theta})|\PauliZ_i|\psi(\bm{\theta})\rangle$
on each wire independently, yielding
$\mathbf{f}\in[-1,1]^N$.
A trainable linear layer $\sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{f}+b)$ maps
$\mathbf{f}$ to a scalar similarity score
$s(\mathbf{x}_a,\mathbf{x}_b)\in(0,1)$.

The use of $N$ local observables rather than a single global observable is
motivated by Cerezo et~al.~\cite{cerezo2021costbarren}, who prove that
local cost gradients vanish at most polynomially in $N$ for shallow circuits,
whereas global cost gradients vanish exponentially as $O(1/2^N)$.

Figure~\ref{fig:circuit} illustrates the complete circuit structure.

\subsection{Hard Negative Mining}
\label{sec:mining}

Random negative sampling produces trivially dissimilar pairs, giving weak
gradient signal~\cite{harwood2017smartmining}.
We identify the two most confusable class pairs by pixel-space class-mean
cosine similarity: bright\_dune/dark\_dune and spider/swiss\_cheese,
consistent with operational HiRISE confusion reports~\cite{wagstaff2021mars}.
During triplet construction, 50\% of negatives are drawn from the confusable
partner class; the remainder are random.
Ablation A4 (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) isolates this effect.

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training}

We use separate Adam optimizers for the CNN encoder
($\alpha_{\mathrm{CNN}} = 10^{-3}$) and the PQC weights
($\alpha_{\mathrm{PQC}} = 10^{-2}$), reflecting the different gradient
magnitudes of classical and quantum parameters.
The post-processing linear layer uses $\alpha_{\mathrm{PQC}}$.

\textbf{Barren plateau monitoring.}
If mean absolute PQC gradient falls below $10^{-5}$ for five consecutive
epochs, the quantum optimizer switches to COBYLA~\cite{powell1994direct};
this fallback was not triggered in any reported experiment.

Training runs for 50 epochs with batch size 16, using a stratified
70/15/15 train/val/test split (per-class, seed 42).
The checkpoint with the highest macro-MAP@10 on the validation set
(evaluated every 5 epochs) is saved; the test split is held out until
final evaluation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

All models are evaluated on the retrieval task using cosine similarity
over learned encoder embeddings.
For a query $q$ with $r_q$ relevant items in the database, the per-query
average precision at $K$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{AP}@K(q) = \frac{1}{\min(K,\,r_q)}
  \sum_{k=1}^{K} P(k)\cdot\mathbf{1}[\text{rank-}k\text{ result is relevant}],
  \label{eq:apk}
\end{equation}
where $P(k)=|\{\text{relevant items in top-}k\}|/k$.
MAP@$K$ is the mean of $\mathrm{AP}@K$ over all test queries;
macro-MAP@$K$ is the arithmetic mean of per-class MAP@$K$ values.
Micro-MAP@10 averages over all queries and is dominated by the ``other''
class (83.6\% of patches).
\textbf{Macro-MAP@10}—the mean of per-class AP@10—gives equal weight to all
eight terrain classes and is the \emph{primary comparison metric}.
We also report Recall@1 and Recall@5.
Classification accuracy (96\% SOTA~\cite{wagstaff2021mars}) is not reported;
it is not comparable to retrieval MAP@$K$.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}

Two baselines share the identical CNN encoder, triplet loss, training
schedule, and evaluation protocol as \qts{}, differing only in the
similarity head.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Classical} (Ablation~A1): the quantum head is replaced by
      a two-layer MLP
      $\mathrm{Linear}(2N,64)\to\mathrm{ReLU}\to\mathrm{Linear}(64,1)\to\mathrm{Sigmoid}$,
      taking the concatenated embedding pair as input
      (${\approx}1{,}100$ parameters vs.\ 48 for the quantum head).
\item \textbf{SLIQ-style} (Ablation~A2): the interleaved encoding is
      replaced by sequential $\RZ(\pi e_a^{(i)})$/$\RX(\pi e_b^{(i)})$
      rotations in two separate encoding blocks, with the ansatz and
      measurements unchanged.
\end{itemize}

This baseline set is designed to isolate the contribution of the
similarity head type (A1) and encoding strategy (A2).
Comparison with standard image-retrieval baselines (e.g., deep
supervised hashing, local-feature retrieval) is an acknowledged
limitation of this study; such comparisons are deferred to future work
and discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:impl}

All experiments are implemented in PyTorch 2.1 with PennyLane~0.45
\cite{bergholm2018pennylane} using the \texttt{default.qubit} statevector
simulator and \texttt{diff\_method=``backprop''} for exact gradient
computation (no shot noise).
The \texttt{TorchLayer} interface wraps the \texttt{QNode} as a
differentiable PyTorch module; embedding slices use
\texttt{inputs[\ldots,:N]} to preserve batch dimensions.
All experiments run on CPU (statevector dimension $2^8{=}256$).

\textbf{ResNet50 baseline.}
ImageNet-pretrained backbone; grayscale replicated to 3 channels
(no first-layer reinitialization); fine-tuned end-to-end with triplet loss
($\alpha_{\text{backbone}}{=}10^{-4}$, $\alpha_{\text{head}}{=}10^{-3}$);
2048$\to N$ linear head with $\ell_2$ normalization; same 50-epoch schedule,
batch size, and split as \qts{}.

\textbf{Variance and replication.}
All results in Tables~\ref{tab:main} and~\ref{tab:ablation} are produced
from a single training run with seed~42.
Differences of $|\Delta| \lesssim 0.01$ macro-MAP@10---notably A1
($\Delta=0.005$) and A4 ($\Delta=0.005$)---are within typical
seed-to-seed variance for models of this complexity and should be
interpreted as directional rather than definitive.
Multi-seed replication ($\geq 3$ seeds per ablation cell) is recommended
before drawing strong conclusions from these small deltas.

% ── 6. Results ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Retrieval Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports retrieval performance on the held-out test
split for all four models.
\qts{} achieves micro-MAP@10 = 0.879, macro-MAP@10 = 0.533,
Recall@1 = 0.856, and Recall@5 = 0.951, ranking first on all metrics.

Notably, ResNet50 achieves the lowest macro-MAP@10 (0.378) despite
${\sim}23\,\text{M}$ ImageNet-pretrained parameters: overhead grayscale
geomorphic structure is orthogonal to natural-image color-texture priors,
and our domain-specific encoder outperforms it by 0.155 macro-MAP@10.

The macro-MAP gap between \qts{} and SLIQ-style encoding ($\Delta = 0.061$)
substantially exceeds the gap versus the classical head ($\Delta = 0.005$).
This asymmetry indicates that the encoding strategy—interleaved
$\RX$/$\RY$ per qubit versus SLIQ's sequential $\RZ$/$\RX$ blocks—is the
dominant factor for minority-class retrieval, while the quantum circuit
itself provides only a marginal advantage over an equivalently parameterized
classical MLP on aggregate micro-MAP.

% ── 7. Discussion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Encoding strategy (A2).}
The $\Delta=0.061$ macro-MAP@10 gap between \qts{} and SLIQ-style encoding
is attributable jointly to interleaved ordering and axis choice: $\RZ|0\rangle$
differs from $|0\rangle$ only by a global phase, so SLIQ's first $\RZ$
block is a no-op for local-$\PauliZ$ measurements, making the baseline
partially degenerate.
A controlled A2.5 condition (sequential $\RX$/$\RY$ at matched axes) would
disentangle these factors and is an immediate priority for follow-up.
The quantum-vs-classical head gap ($\Delta=0.005$, A1) is within typical
seed-to-seed variance at a single run and is treated as directional only.

\textbf{Domain specificity and class imbalance.}
ResNet50's poor macro-MAP@10 (0.378) reflects a domain gap: overhead
grayscale geomorphic patches share little with ImageNet's color-texture
prior, and the 83.6\% ``other'' class means micro-MAP@10 ($>$0.82 for all
models) masks the full spread.
Macro-MAP@10 reveals the true picture: a 1.7\,M-parameter domain-specific
encoder outperforms the 23\,M-parameter baseline by 0.155.
Future work should explore stratified triplet construction or
embedding-proximity mining~\cite{harwood2017smartmining} to further reduce
class-frequency bias.

\textbf{Ablation findings (A3--A5).}
Circuit depth (A3) has a non-monotone effect: $L=3$ (0.556) outperforms
$L=1$ (0.538) and the default $L=2$ (0.533), suggesting additional CZ
layers improve minority-class discriminability; practitioners should
prefer $L=3$.
Hard negative mining (A4, $\Delta=0.005$ favoring random mining) is
within seed-to-seed variance at a single run and is treated as directional.
Qubit count (A5) shows macro-MAP@10 decreasing with $N$: $N=4$ (0.540),
$N=8$ (0.533), $N=12$ (0.515), likely because larger $N$ simultaneously
increases encoder output dimension and variational parameter count, making
optimization harder within 50 epochs.
Evaluating the combination of $L=3$ with random mining at $N=8$ yields
0.546—between $L=3$ alone (0.556) and random mining alone (0.538)—indicating
a sub-additive interaction between these factors.
The full combined configuration at $N=4$ remains an avenue for future work.

\textbf{Limitations.}
All results are single-run estimates (seed~42); $|\Delta|\lesssim0.01$
should be treated as directional pending multi-seed replication.
The A2 comparison is partially confounded by the $\RZ$-on-$|0\rangle$
degeneracy; an A2.5 axis-matched control is needed.
Standard image-retrieval baselines (deep supervised hashing,
local-feature retrieval) are absent; the current comparison isolates
the quantum head and encoding strategy only.
Operational HiRISE retrieval would require full-resolution tiled queries,
and end-to-end PDS integration is deferred to future work.

% ── 8. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented \qts{}, a quantum metric learning architecture for
planetary terrain image retrieval.
A Siamese CNN encoder paired with an entangled-pair PQC similarity head
achieves macro-MAP@10 = 0.533 on the HiRISE~v3 held-out test split
($N{=}8$, $L{=}2$), with $L{=}3$ reaching 0.556—the best result across
all ablation conditions.
The encoding strategy is the dominant factor: interleaved $\RX$/$\RY$
rotations outperform SLIQ-style sequential encoding by $\Delta=0.061$
macro-MAP@10, while local Pauli-$\PauliZ$ measurements suppress barren
plateaus throughout training.
Notably, an ImageNet-pretrained ResNet50 achieves the lowest macro-MAP@10
of all models (0.378), confirming that domain-specific metric learning
outperforms large-scale transfer for minority planetary terrain classes.
Immediate next steps include multi-seed replication, evaluating the
$N{=}4$, $L{=}3$, random mining configuration, and scaling to hardware
backends for $N>12$; a longer-range direction is cross-instrument terrain
matching against lower-resolution HiRISE archives (CTX, CaSSIS).
